A guide

How to make a Zachlike

A Zachlike is an engineering puzzle game. The player builds a machine, runs it, watches it fail, and fixes it. This is how to make one.

You are aiming for one thing: a small set of understandable rules whose interactions produce a large space of solutions, presented so the player can see every consequence.

Both halves matter. Rules that interact but stay hidden produce cleverness the player cannot check. Rules that are visible but never interact produce a list of chores.

Who this is for

Someone who wants to make one of these and has not made one before. It assumes you can build software, or can work with someone who can. It assumes nothing about puzzle design, and nothing about the genre — §1 starts by describing what one of these games is actually like to play.

You do not need a large team, a physics engine, or an art budget. Most games in this genre were made by one to five people, and most of the work is deciding what to leave out. The hard parts are not technical: they are choosing a small enough rule set, writing puzzles that admit more than one answer, and making the machine explain itself when it fails.

1 · What a Zachlike is

If you have played one, skim this and go to §2. If you have not, everything after this chapter will read as abstractions about a thing you have never seen. So here is the thing.

Five minutes inside one

A message arrives from your employer. It says a client needs six units of orange pigment, that you have red and yellow on tap, and that anything you build has to fit on the bench. That message is the brief G006 — the in-fiction statement of the job. Stripped of its voice, the requirements it contains are the spec G033: what goes in, what must come out, how many, and what you are not allowed to exceed.

You are looking at a grid. That is the board G004 — the space you build in. Down one side is a row of parts you are allowed to use: pipe segments, a mixer, a vat. That is the palette G021. Not every part in the game — the ones offered for this job.

Nobody tells you how to do it. You drag a pipe from the red tap, another from the yellow tap, point both into the mixer, run a pipe from the mixer to the output, and press Run.

The machine starts moving. Time advances in discrete steps — ticks G035 — and one unit of pigment travels one pipe segment per tick. Red arrives at the mixer. Yellow arrives a tick later, because you made that pipe one segment longer. The mixer sits there holding red, waiting. It is not broken and it has not crashed; it is blocked G003, and the game shows you that as a state on the part itself, not as an error message.

Then yellow lands, the mixer works for two ticks, and orange comes out. It travels to the output. One down, five to go.

But something is wrong further back. The red tap has kept producing, the pipe behind the mixer has filled up, and now the tap itself has stopped. That is backpressure G002 — a full destination stalling its own supply, all the way up the chain. You can see it: the pipe is visibly packed with red units that have nowhere to go. Nothing told you this would happen. You worked it out by watching.

The run finishes anyway. Backpressure is not a crash — the taps outrun the mixer no matter what you build, so the pipes behind it were always going to fill, and a full pipe is just a full pipe. What it is telling you is which part is the slow one. The queue is sitting behind the mixer, so the mixer is the problem.

The game tells you it took 28 cycles G009, used 11 tiles of bench, and wasted 11 units of pigment — every unit drawn off a tap that was still standing in a pipe when the sixth orange landed. Those are the metrics G019. Below them is a chart of everyone else's cycle counts — a histogram G017 — and you are well to the right of the crowd. Somebody solved this in 20.

You press Reset and drag the yellow pipe one segment shorter, so both colours arrive together and the mixer stops waiting a tick on every batch. 24 cycles, 10 tiles. Better, and still nowhere near 20.

You had been about to move on. Now you are not.

What just happened

That was the entire genre, in miniature.

The loop is build → run → inspect → edit. You made a thing, watched it execute, found where your reasoning was wrong, and changed it. Every part of the game exists to serve that loop or it does not belong.

The puzzle described what, not how. Nothing in the game holds a correct answer to compare yours against; it checks whether six units of orange came out. Somebody else's machine, laid out completely differently, passes just as well. That is not a nicety — it is the mechanical reason this genre has a culture of comparing solutions.

The failure explained itself. The jam was visible, in the place it happened, at the moment it happened. You never had to ask which hidden rule fired.

Correct came before optimal. Your clumsy 28-cycle machine finished the puzzle. The score is an invitation, not a gate. That split — solving is for everyone, optimizing is for whoever wants it — is what lets one game serve both a casual player and someone who will spend nine hours shaving four cycles.

What it is not

The boundary is worth drawing, because the near neighbours pull designs apart:

  • Not a logic puzzle with one hidden answer. The player builds, and many builds pass.
  • Not a factory game. Space and time are scarce, and "build bigger" is not available.
  • Not a coding tutorial. The language is deliberately poor; the poverty is the puzzle.
  • Not a reflex game. Nothing here is about pressing a button at the right moment.

Go and play two of them

You cannot design for a genre you have not felt. Appendix E lists the games this guide is drawn from and what to take from each; here is what you actually do in the ones worth starting with:

2 · What you are making

§1 described one of these from the outside. This chapter is the same thing stated as design commitments — the small number of things that have to be true, and the two that break quietly if you are not watching for them. It is the shortest complete answer to "what am I actually signing up for?"

The loop

Build → run → inspect → edit. The player places parts, presses Run, watches, finds the fault, changes something. Everything else — story, scores, progression — hangs off that loop.

If the loop is not enjoyable with placeholder art and three parts, no amount of content will save it. P004

What players love

  • The first clean run that needs no supervision.
  • Compression: the same behaviour in less space, time, or code.
  • A machine legible enough to screenshot — the picture is the proof of skill.
  • The choreography of a mature machine running.
  • Beating the hump on a score distribution.
  • Realising they invented a latch, a pipeline, a mutex without being told the name.

What players hate

  • Failing a hidden test case after passing every visible one.
  • Having to demolish a working machine to change it.
  • A collision at cycle 400 of a 401-cycle run.
  • Requirements buried in fiction or a dense datasheet.
  • Guessing what the designer wanted.
  • A deadlock with no explanation.

Seven rules

They protect the first list and prevent the second. Break one deliberately if you must; do not break one by accident.

  1. The player builds something that runs. The same object is built, executed, inspected, debugged, revised, optimized, saved, and shared. P001
  2. The puzzle says what, not how. The spec describes behaviour at the boundary. Two completely different machines must both pass if they satisfy it. P009 · L001
  3. Correctness is judged on behaviour. You test what the machine does, never whether it resembles a hidden reference. P002 · M001
  4. Execution is deterministic, discrete, and inspectable. Same machine, same input, same sequence, every time. Time, order, state, waiting, blocking, and failure can all be looked at. P005
  5. Failure explains itself. A failed run points at the earliest thing that went wrong and keeps enough state to inspect it. It answers "where was my reasoning wrong?", never "which hidden rule fired?" P007
  6. Correct beats optimal. A messy but honest machine passes. Progression never requires a good score unless the score is the explicit subject of that puzzle. P003 · L004
  7. The machine is the trophy. Solving ends with watching the thing run cleanly and keeping it. A results screen must not hide what the player made. P061

Two rules that break quietly

Hidden test cases. Multiple and adversarial cases are good design — they stop players hard-coding answers and show the real abstraction by example M002 · P010. But failing a hidden case after passing every visible one is the single most-cited frustration in the genre. The fix is rule 3: hidden cases must be inferable from the visible ones. If a player who reasoned correctly from what you showed them can still fail, your spec was incomplete, not their thinking.

Scores as gates. A hard limit defines validity. A score invites refinement. The moment a score becomes a gate, rule 6 is broken and the game stops being finishable by ordinary players.

Two things to reject

General game design advice endorses both of these. Here they break the loop.

Randomness inside the puzzle loop. It is admissible in generated content or in explicitly optional robustness tests, where the player can understand the distribution and reproduce a failure. It is never admissible in core validation, and never as a difficulty knob. Determinism is what makes prediction possible, debugging honest, and shared solutions meaningful.

Real-time or dexterity pressure. This is the genre's best-documented mistake. SpaceChem — the game that founded the form, and otherwise an excellent one — put real-time, randomised boss fights on the mandatory path; roughly 2% of tracked players reached the final puzzle. Its designers have said so publicly and at length (Appendix F). Your player is thinking, not reacting.

The five layers, and the one around them

The rest of the guide is built on this. Each layer answers one question, and each is a place to look when something is wrong. The five are the next five chapters, in order; presentation is the sixth chapter and is not a sixth layer. §4 works a small game through all of them before you get there.

Layer Question it answers
Domain What process is being simulated?
Mechanics What can the player do?
Systems What emerges when the mechanics interact?
Puzzles What must the player produce, under what conditions?
Optimization How can a working solution be made better?
Presentation — around all five How is all of it made understandable?

Domain → Mechanics → Systems → Puzzles → Optimization

The first five form a chain, and presentation surrounds all of them — it cannot create depth, but it reveals, amplifies, or hides it.

The chain is a way to locate a problem, not a production schedule. You will move in both directions: prototypes expose the real system, puzzles reveal missing or dominant mechanics, optimization shows whether your trade-offs are real, and any of those can send you back to redesign a mechanic or the domain itself.

When two good ideas conflict, this is the order. Player trust — determinism, fairness, consistent rules — first. Then the experience you intended. Then the quality of the build–run–inspect–edit loop. Then clarity. Then meaningful choice, accessibility, pacing, content quantity, and spectacle, in that order. Nothing outranks the player's ability to trust the simulator.

3 · Build the smallest version first

Your first build is not a game. It is one puzzle that runs.

Make a board G004, three or four parts, one spec G033, and the buttons Run, Step, and Reset. Placeholder art. No story, no scores, no menu. Then solve your own puzzle and see whether you enjoyed it.

This is the genre's version of a vertical slice G039, and it is unusually cheap here because almost none of the cost of a Zachlike is in its art. A weekend is a realistic budget. §4 is what one of these looks like written down.

The thing you are testing is not whether the puzzle is good. It is whether the loop is worth repeating — whether pressing Run on your own machine and watching it fail is interesting enough that you want to fix it. If that is not true with three parts, it will not become true with thirty.

Do this. Once it works, make two more puzzles out of the same parts, each pressuring something different. If you cannot get three genuinely different puzzles out of your parts, the parts are the problem, not the puzzles — go back to Mechanics.

Before you go into production

A prototype has done its job when you can watch someone else play and see all of this:

  • They understand the immediate goal without being told.
  • The central act is enjoyable without final art.
  • Finishing one puzzle makes them want the next.
  • A real decision happens every minute or so.
  • Important actions have obvious feedback.
  • Failure is understandable and retrying is fast.
  • The experience you intended shows up while you watch, not while you explain.
  • Playtests turn up fixable problems rather than rejection of the idea.
  • You can name what you would cut to protect the scope.

If some of these are missing, the fix is in the system, not in more content. Write the one-page definition before full production — not before the prototype. You learn more in a week of building than in a month of planning, and the plan is better afterwards.

4 · A worked example · the Dye Bench

The rest of this guide is organised as five layers, and each of them is easier to understand with something concrete underneath it. So here is one small game, designed all the way through. It is the bench you played in §1, seen from behind.

It is deliberately tiny — one afternoon of design, a weekend of building. Later chapters come back to it whenever an abstract rule needs an instance. Nothing else depends on its specifics; if you already have a domain in mind, substitute yours as you go.

Layer 1 · The domain

Mixing and dispensing pigment. A workshop that takes raw colour off a tap, combines it, and fills orders.

The reason to pick a process rather than a look is that a process hands you your verbs for free. Ask what someone in a dye works actually does, and you get: pour, mix, heat, dispense. Ask what limits them, and you get: how much a vessel holds, how far a pipe reaches, how long a batch takes to come up to temperature, how much pigment you are throwing away. Every one of those is a mechanic waiting to be drawn.

"A game about colour" would have given you none of that. See §5.

Layer 2 · The mechanics

Four parts, plus fixed taps and an output port:

Part Rule The decision it creates
Pipe segment Directional. Holds one unit. Moves it one segment per tick Routing, and timing — length is delay
Mixer Consumes one unit from each of two inputs, waits two ticks, emits the blend Synchronisation: both inputs must arrive together
Vat Holds up to four units, first in first out. Feeds any tile it touches Whether to buffer — and it is the only part that can supply two things at once
Burner Consumes one unit, waits three ticks, emits it darkened Ordering: darken the ingredients, or darken the result?

That is four parts, and one of them is questionable. The burner has exactly the same shape as the mixer — consume, wait, emit — so it may not be a second mechanic at all, just the mixer with one input instead of two. §6 settles that argument properly; the short version is that it earns its place only because darkening before mixing and darkening after mixing produce genuinely different machines.

Note what is not here. No pumps, no valves, no filters, no temperature gauge. Four parts is enough to be going on with, and the discipline of §6 is that a fifth has to argue its way in.

Layer 3 · The system

The eight rules that never change, for every puzzle on this bench:

  • A unit of pigment is one tile, one colour.
  • Colour changes only inside a mixer or a burner.
  • Pipes are directional; a pipe feeds whatever tile it points into.
  • Time is discrete ticks; everything resolves at once.
  • Two units entering one tile is a collision — the run fails there and stops.
  • A pipe holds one unit and feeds exactly one tile; a vat holds four and feeds any tile it touches.
  • A run fails on collision, or ends wrong if the output is not what the spec asked for.
  • Editing happens while stopped, never mid-run.

From those eight rules, behaviour appears that none of them mentions. A full vat stops the pipe behind it, which stops the pipe behind that, all the way back to the tap — backpressure G002, which is what jammed your first attempt in §1. Nobody designed backpressure. It falls out of "a pipe holds one unit" plus "everything resolves at once", and it is the single most useful thing on this bench, because it makes a stalled machine show you where it stalled. See §7.

Layer 4 · A puzzle

SPEC six units of orange to the output port
INPUTS red tap, yellow tap — endless, fixed positions
BOARD 8 × 6 bench
PALETTE pipe · mixer · vat
LIMITS none — the bench edge is the only bound

The bottleneck this puzzle exists to pressure is synchronisation: the mixer needs both colours in the same tick, and the two taps are not the same distance away. Everything else about it is easy on purpose.

The obvious solution passes. Two pipes, one mixer, one pipe out. It works, it is what almost everyone builds, and it runs in 24 cycles on 10 tiles. The mixer is a batch vessel — it will not take a fresh load until the last one has left — so it spends one tick in every three reloading, and six units of orange cost eighteen ticks of mixer plus the time to fill and drain the pipes. Everything the machine does is paced by that one part.

The better solution is a reframing, not a trick. Two mixers working in alternation, so one is always mixing while the other loads. It runs in 20 cycles and takes 21 tiles — over twice the bench, because each mixer needs its own approach and the taps have to be split to reach both. Nothing new had to be learned to see it: it is made entirely of parts already on the palette, and it makes complete sense in hindsight. That is the test in §8.

Splitting the taps is the part worth dwelling on. A pipe feeds exactly one tile, so the only way to send red to two mixers is to put a vat in the way — and it takes one vat per colour, not one shared between them, because a vat hands out whatever reached it first and a mixer needs one of each. Nobody wrote that rule either. It is what "a pipe feeds one tile" means once you want two of something.

Note where the alternation comes from, too. Nobody wrote a rule that says a vat takes turns. Because a vat can only push into a branch that is empty, and each branch stays occupied for exactly as long as its mixer is busy, the turn-taking falls out on its own. That is the same kind of behaviour as the jam — authored nowhere, and the reason §7 is a separate layer.

Note what is not used to make this hard. The quota is six, not sixty. Raising it to sixty would add four minutes of watching and not one second of thinking.

Six is close to the floor, though, and it is worth knowing why. The parallel build wins on throughput and loses on the time it takes to fill and drain; at six units those nearly cancel, and it comes out four cycles ahead. At twelve it is thirteen ahead. A quota has to be long enough for the difference between two architectures to show up at all — that, and not difficulty, is what a quota is for. See §8.

Layer 5 · The metrics

Cycles, bench tiles, and pigment wasted — every unit drawn off a tap that never reaches the output.

They have to genuinely fight, so argue it pair by pair — and then go and measure it on the two builds above, because the argument is easy to win in your head and easy to lose on the bench:

Pair Serial → parallel Do they conflict?
Cycles vs. tiles 24 → 20 cycles, 10 → 21 tiles Yes. Going faster means running mixers in parallel, and every extra mixer is bench you no longer have
Cycles vs. waste 24 → 20 cycles, 8 → 28 wasted Yes. Two mixers means two primed approaches, and primed pipes are full of pigment still sitting there when the run ends
Tiles vs. waste 10 → 21 tiles, 8 → 28 wasted No. They rise together. A bigger machine is a machine with more pigment stranded in it, every time

The third row is the finding, and it is the one the argument-in-your-head got wrong: tiles and waste do not trade off, they correlate. §9 is blunt about what that means — a pair that moves together is a pair you are not really scoring on, and you have a two-axis puzzle wearing three axes. The fix is either to give waste teeth of its own, by charging for the taps so that idling costs pigment whether or not the machine is big, or to stop advertising three.

Two honest axes out of three is the normal result of doing this properly. Getting three would have been the surprise.

What this is not

One puzzle is not a game. There is no progression here, no second puzzle, no story, no reason the player is dyeing anything for anybody. Those come later and they come from watching someone play this one. What this is is the thing §3 tells you to build first: small enough to finish, real enough to prove the loop is worth playing.

Do this. Write your own version of this chapter for your own domain, on one page, before you build anything. Four parts, eight rules, one puzzle, three metrics, and the pair-by-pair argument that the metrics conflict. If you cannot fill in the metrics table honestly, you have found a real problem while it is still free to fix.

5 · Layer 1 · Domain

The domain is the process your game simulates — the slice of reality, engineering, or invented physics the player learns to manipulate. It is not the setting or the art style. It is where the mechanics come from.

Alchemical manufacturing, molecular engineering, distributed computation, industrial assembly, and mutable grammar are domains. A colour scheme is not.

This is the layer people skip, because it looks like a theming decision and themes feel like something you can settle later. It is not one. Skip it and you will find yourself inventing mechanics from nothing, one at a time, with no way to tell whether the next one belongs — and no answer when a player asks why a part behaves the way it does.

On the Dye Bench. The domain is not "colour" — it is a dye works, a process someone performs. That is what handed over pour, mix, heat and dispense without anyone having to invent them, and what makes a vat holding four units feel like a fact about the world rather than a rule imposed by a designer (§4).

What a good domain gives you

  • Verbs. Molecular engineering suggests bonding, splitting, rotating, transporting. Distributed computation suggests storing, transmitting, waiting, synchronising, branching.
  • Constraints. Space, latency, energy, capacity, reach, heat, scarcity. These feel fair when they come out of the simulated process instead of being imposed on it.
  • Representations. Ports, tracks, instruction lines, atoms, packets, mechanical arms — the concrete things that make abstract rules readable.

Test your domain

  • What process is the player learning to manipulate?
  • Which objects, transformations, and constraints fall out of it naturally?
  • Does the domain generate mechanics, or just redecorate familiar ones?
  • Can the player build useful intuitions from the metaphor?
  • Where must the representation depart from realism to stay clear?

Fix the fiction early, and use it as an oracle. When you are unsure how something should work, ask how it would work in the world. This is the most consistently reported working method in the genre. It is not decoration — it is how a list of puzzles acquires a reason to exist (§12).

You have a domain when you can name the process in a short phrase; when four or five verbs fall out of it without effort; when at least two constraints feel like facts about the world rather than rules you imposed; and when you can answer "how should this behave?" by asking what would happen in the world instead of by deciding.

Next: §6 turns those verbs into a small set of parts.

6 · Layer 2 · Mechanics

A mechanic is a rule, not a themed object. A conveyor, a wire, a pipe, and a queue can all be the same mechanic if they create the same decisions. You have a second mechanic only when a rule changes what the player decides, what they must predict, how they can fail, or what they can optimize.

Representation still matters. The same rule drawn as a wire and as a pipe can differ enormously in how easy it is to read and edit. Same decisions, different experience — which is why Presentation is part of the design and not the art pass.

Most games in this genre run on a handful of rules and a dozen or so parts. Aim low.

That instruction is the hard one to follow, and the failure it prevents is specific: a game where every puzzle needs another exception explained, where what the player learned in the first act stops helping them in the third, and where the manual grows faster than the number of things worth building. That is §14's first row, and it starts here, with a designer who kept saying yes.

On the Dye Bench. Four parts, and the argument was about the fourth. The burner has the same shape as the mixer — consume, wait, emit — so on the "is this a themed object or a rule?" test it looks like a duplicate. It survives for one reason: darkening the ingredients and darkening the blend are different machines, so it changes what the player decides. Had it merely been a mixer that took one input instead of two, it should have been cut (§4).

Five questions to ask of a mechanic

The question is never "is this interesting?" It is "does this make the whole system deeper?"

Criterion Question What to watch for
Domain fit Does it feel like a consequence of the process you are simulating? A gate, not a measure of depth. A good mechanic can still fail here by importing a second metaphor.
Interaction density How many other mechanics does it meaningfully change? Count strategic interactions, not formal connections. A real one changes what can be built, how the player reasons, or what can be optimized.
Tension What must the player give up to use it? Speed against space, simplicity against throughput, flexibility against efficiency. If one use is almost always right, there is no tension.
Local predictability Can an experienced player predict its next effect before pressing Run? Complexity should come from composition, never from uncertainty about a single rule.
Expressiveness Does it enable genuinely new structures? A check, not a score. A mechanic that permits many arrangements without producing decisions is permissive, not deep.

Then four fast follow-ups: does it support several distinct puzzle patterns, does it create new ways to improve a working solution, can its combinations surprise a player and still make sense in hindsight, and will it still matter late in the campaign?

Cut it when one use is obviously best, when it touches only one narrow subsystem, when it duplicates an existing mechanic without changing decisions, when it needs more explanation than it creates possibility, or when it requires an exception to your core rules.

Nine families to choose from

This is the genre's design space. It is a menu, not a shopping list — pick a small, coherent subset. The IDs open the full mechanics register.

Do not read the table looking for good ideas. Read it once to see the shape of the space, then work backwards: your domain from §5 already implies one or two families, and the useful question is which second family crosses well with the one you are obviously in. The Dye Bench is family B, flow and capacity, with a little of C — and knowing that is what makes "should there be a burner?" a decidable question rather than a matter of taste.

Family IDs Central question Examples
A · Specification & verification M001–010, M103, M113 Can the player infer a fair spec and test against it? Spec-driven I/O; adversarial cases; quotas; restricted palettes; execution traces
B · Flow & capacity networks M011–M029 How do topology, capacity, and timing make movement a design problem? Directed channels; splitting and merging; buffers; backpressure; signalling; crossings
C · Transformation grammars M030–M044 Can a small vocabulary of operations compose into several routes from input to output? Multi-input processes; cutting; rotation; joining; ordered dependencies; byproducts
D · Spatial construction M045–M060 Does geometry create real access, routing, and timing decisions? Grid, hex, 3D, graph placement; reach-limited arms; collision; gravity; finite inventories
E · Time & concurrency M061–M073 Can the player understand how local schedules produce global behaviour? Global ticks; per-device timelines; blocking communication; timers; races; deadlock
F · Programming & information M074–M093 How much behaviour can the player express with a deliberately poor language? Minimal instruction sets; limited state; environment as memory; mobile agents; subroutines
G · Rule manipulation & deduction M094–M104 Is the player manipulating objects, or the rules themselves? Movable rule tokens; dynamic properties; mutable objectives; self-reference; constraint propagation
H · Optimization & architecture M105–M117 Do different priorities produce visibly different machines? Speed, cost, area, energy metrics; robustness margins; modularity; scaling under load
I · Progression, pressure & meta M118–M135 Does this deepen construction, or compete with it? Capability unlocks; branching progression; faults; sandboxes and sharing; in-world diversions

Two families carry warnings. Rule manipulation (G) is powerful but attacks the stable core the player relies on — make it foundational or leave it out, never a late exception. Progression pressure (I) includes real-time pressure, random failure, dexterity, and adversarial play. Those are fine in other engineering games. Do not let them into a contemplative one by accident.

To find something genuinely new, cross two families with strong domain fit, state how each changes the decisions the other creates, and test the result against the five questions above. Reject it if it is merely novel.

What is your player short of?

The families say what a mechanic is. This says what your game is about. Most games commit to one or two, and choosing deliberately gets you to a coherent design faster than collecting mechanics one at a time.

Read the middle column first. Whatever your player is short of is what your game is about — and if the honest answer is "nothing in particular", that is the finding. A player who is short of nothing has no problem to solve, only work to do.

Time as fabric Schedule: cycles, phase, collision windows Sequencing — correctness lives in the schedule, not the structure
Language poverty Vocabulary: instructions, registers, states Expressing a rich task with poor means
Flow networks Capacity: everything is a graph with limits Routing and balancing
Growth loops Nothing, structurally — solving produces more solving at larger scale Scaling. The furthest pole from puzzle design
Adversarial reality Certainty: hidden cases, faults, pressure Robustness rather than cleverness
Reflexive rules Stability: the system can change the system Reasoning about semantics, not objects
Social exhibition Distinction: scores, distributions, shareable machines Making a solution worth showing

You have your mechanics when you can list them on one hand; when each one survives the five questions above rather than being there because it seemed fun; when you can say in one sentence what your player is short of; and when adding the next part would require you to argue for it rather than just draw it.

7 · Layer 3 · Systems

A system is what emerges when mechanics interact: pipelines, clocks, state machines, production loops, routing architectures, feedback. You have a real system when one mechanic changes the strategic value of another, local actions have global consequences, the same parts support several architectures, and simple rules produce behaviour you have to simulate rather than read off.

This is the layer you cannot author directly, which is what makes it strange to work on. You write rules; the system is what those rules turn out to do together. Emergence G014 is the word for behaviour that appears without anyone writing it down — like the jam in §1, which nobody designed. Your job here is not to invent the system but to set up conditions in which a good one can exist, and then go and look at what you actually got.

Decide the core rules once

These are the rules that never change, for the whole campaign: P014

  • What counts as an object.
  • How objects keep their identity.
  • How connections work.
  • How time advances.
  • How simultaneous actions resolve.
  • How capacity is represented.
  • How failure is detected.
  • How editing affects a running machine.

Later mechanics extend this grammar. A new part should be a new verb in the existing grammar, not a new grammar. Repeated exceptions destroy transfer, and transfer is what makes mastery feel cumulative.

Design interactions, not inventories

For every pair of core mechanics, ask: do they affect each other? Does the combination create a new decision? Does one change the cost or value of the other? Can they combine in more than one way? Is the interaction visible?

A mechanic-interaction matrix is the cheapest instrument in this guide and the one most often skipped. It shows you isolated parts and dominant combinations before you have built a hundred puzzles on top of them.

It is also less work than it sounds. Four parts is six pairs; here is the whole thing for the Dye Bench, written in about ten minutes:

Pair What the combination creates Strength
Pipe × Mixer Pipe length is delay, so the mixer's synchronisation problem is a geometry problem Strong
Pipe × Vat A vat absorbs timing slack that a pipe cannot — and since a pipe feeds one tile, a vat is the only way to supply two things at once Strong
Mixer × Vat Buffering lets a mixer run continuously instead of in lockstep with its supply Strong
Mixer × Burner Darken the ingredients or darken the blend — two orders, two machines Strong
Vat × Burner A vat in front of a burner smooths its three-tick latency Medium
Pipe × Burner Delay followed by delay. No new decision Weak

The weak cell is the finding. The burner is worth having only because of what it does with the mixer — so if the mixer ever leaves, the burner goes with it. That is a useful thing to know before rather than after authoring thirty puzzles, and no amount of thinking about the burner on its own would have surfaced it.

Protect determinism

The simulator must be impartial and inspectable. Define these and keep them stable: the unit of time, update order, collision order, communication and blocking rules, tie-breaking, test-case order, score calculation. When you change a rule, say so in the patch notes — a silent change invalidates designs players reasoned their way to. P060

Determinism G012 — the same machine, on the same input, behaving identically every single time — is what makes prediction possible, debugging honest, and shared solutions meaningful. Every failure is the player's, so every success is too.

Keep causality traceable

Objects, values, and control stay followable. A meaningful transfer has a visible path or an explicit transition. If your abstraction includes instant communication, show its endpoints and its moment of activation. Teleportation is fine only when the teleport rule is itself visible. Transformations happen at fixed, marked sites, with inputs, preconditions, and outputs inspectable where the change occurs. P025 · P021

Make constraints into objects

Wherever you can, a limit should be a thing on screen rather than a rule in a manual: P013

  • capacity → slots
  • reach → geometry
  • bandwidth → ports or lanes
  • memory → visible cells or lines
  • time → a tick or a trace
  • cost → an explicit counter
  • collision → occupied space
  • energy → visible consumption

The test: can the player predict whether an edit is legal, and what it will cost, before committing it?

Where global complexity should come from

Good sources: shared capacity, synchronisation, feedback, alternate transformation routes, irreversible operations, limited state, topology G036 — what connects to what, regardless of where it sits — trade-offs that are not monotone, and several valid decompositions.

Bad sources, all of which read as unfairness: hidden state, inconsistent exceptions, visual ambiguity, and sheer scale.

Two behaviours worth naming, because your players will meet both and will need words for them. Backpressure G002 is a full destination stalling its own supply back up the chain; it is a gift, because a machine that clogs visibly is a machine that shows you where it went wrong. Deadlock G010 is two parts each waiting on something the other holds, so everything stops with nothing visibly wrong; it is the opposite of a gift, and if your system can produce one you must detect it and say so out loud.

You have a system when the eight core rules above are written down and you have stopped changing them; when the interaction matrix has more strong cells than weak ones; when at least one behaviour appears that you did not author; and when you can state what happens on a tie, on a collision, and on a full container without having to go and read the code.

8 · Layer 4 · Puzzles

Puzzles are the game. The list of them is what the player does for twenty hours, and the genre's most famous failure was a level-design failure, not an art or story one.

A puzzle is a spec G033 plus a board G004 plus a palette G021. The spec says what must come out. The board is the space you build in. The palette is the parts you are given — the ones offered for this job, not every part in the game. Those three are your entire difficulty toolkit.

Three dials is fewer than it sounds like, and the discipline of this chapter is mostly about not reaching for a fourth. The fourth one people reach for is the quota, and it does not work.

Design the problem, never the solution

Write the spec — inputs, required outputs, test cases, quota, hard limits — and deliberately do not write a solution. The engine checks output, not method. Nothing in the game knows an intended answer. That is the mechanical reason many solutions can exist. L001

It is also faster to make: designing a puzzle and its clever solution at the same time is a chicken-and-egg problem, and designing only the problem is not.

Do this. Write the target and the palette first. Then check that several genuinely different builds pass. If you catch yourself protecting one clever layout, widen the spec.

Say what, not how

The brief states what must be produced, from which inputs, how much, which limits bind, and what counts as failure. It does not list construction steps or teach part behaviour — that is the interface's job and the manual's job (§11).

The review test: does the brief leave more than one plausible starting architecture? P009

Guarantee a solution exists — never ship one

Two models; pick per puzzle. With unbounded resources, the puzzle is solvable by construction and all the difficulty moves into the metrics. With bounded resources, you have to verify solvability, and the tightness itself is the challenge.

Verify with hand-built reference solutions G027, exhaustive search, a constraint solver, or structured playtesting. Ship the guarantee, never the answer key. L006

Let the obvious solution pass

A fair puzzle has a naive solution that works and scores badly, plus a better reframing that improves a metric. The insight must be built from primitives you already taught, and it must make complete sense in hindsight. It is a new way to see constraints the player already holds, not a mechanic they had to guess exists. L005

Mix the two kinds of puzzle. An Aha! puzzle G001 hides the answer behind an insight. A process puzzle G025 is understood immediately and takes work. All-process is tedious; all-Aha shuts people out. The general shape: make it obvious what to do and not obvious how.

Do this. Let the obvious decomposition G011 — the way the player splits the problem into pieces — pass and score poorly, then put the elegant version where the bad score sends the player looking: a subtraction instead of an addition, a shared intermediate, a factored target. That is how an insight becomes discoverable without becoming guessable.

On the Dye Bench. The obvious build is two pipes into one mixer, and it passes — 24 cycles on 10 tiles. The reframing is two mixers alternating, which runs in 20 but takes 21 tiles. Nothing new had to be learned to see the second one; it is made of parts already on the palette, and the only thing that sends a player looking for it is the sight of their own 24 sitting to the right of everyone else's (§4).

Name the bottleneck before you build

Harder does not mean bigger. Say out loud which facet this puzzle exists to pressure — this is the puzzle's bottleneck G005 — from routing, synchronisation, storage, geometry, bandwidth, decomposition, state, communication, collision, transformation order, or topology. If the honest answer is "the last puzzle, but more", it is a duplicate — re-aim it or cut it. L007

Raising a quota G026 — how many outputs the puzzle demands — is the classic fake difficulty. Quotas exist to make the machine run long enough for timing to bite, not to create throughput G034 pressure. Keep them small. Six units of orange is a puzzle; sixty is the same puzzle plus four minutes of watching.

The board is your main difficulty dial

You have three choices here and they are not interchangeable — each one moves the difficulty to a different place, and the third column is the part to read:

Board Effect Where the difficulty lives
Bounded and cramped The constraint is the puzzle; few layouts fit Solving. Variety comes from radically different ways to squeeze under the limit
Open canvas Any honest solution works Optimizing. Easy to finish, hard to master
Graph or network Coordination between nodes is the problem Communication topology and protocol

The sweet spot for reaching both casual and obsessive players is an open canvas with a score distribution. Default to generously bounded unless the bounds are the puzzle. L008

There is a real line between "tight and clever" and "tight and unfair". Cramping has to buy a cleverer decomposition; if it only makes the obvious solution barely fit, it is a packing chore.

But boundedness is what stops a puzzle game decaying into a factory game. With unlimited space, the answer to every shortfall is "build bigger", and problem-solving turns into waiting for throughput.

Do this. Set board and palette to place the solve/optimize split where you want it, rather than by habit. When a puzzle is too easy, tighten the board or thin the palette — never raise the quota.

Treat the palette as a balance lever

Ship the parts the bottleneck needs plus near neighbours, and nothing else. Adding a part opens new decompositions and can quietly trivialise your bottleneck, so every palette change is a balance change. M007 · P066

The lever cuts both ways. Too loose trivialises. Too tight funnels — a palette that admits only one architecture turns an open puzzle into "guess what the designer wanted", which is the same failure as validating against a hidden answer.

Make the thinking the cost, not the labour

Watch the shape of solve time, not just its length. If the player finds the idea in a minute and then spends an hour repeating placements, shifting a large structure by one cell, unrolling code by hand, or waiting through long runs, your difficulty is clerical. L011

Fix it with better structural editing — copy and paste regions, multi-select, retiming, deep undo — or by reshaping the puzzle so the effort stays in the reasoning.

Author two solution families

A solution family G032 is a group of solutions that share an architecture — members differ in detail, families differ in idea. A strong puzzle supports more than cosmetic variation: different decompositions, different topologies, serial against parallel, different uses of space or state, designs that specialise in one metric. Two genuine families beat ten rearrangements of one — and they are what makes a score distribution grow a second hump (§9).

Order by watching, and keep walls off the path

Difficulty order is edited after observation, never authored by intuition. Sketch the set, estimate, watch a small group play, then do three things: reorder by measured difficulty, delete puzzles whose insight you already taught, and insert a bridge where the jump is too large. A drop-off in completion is design evidence, not player failure. L009

Open-ended difficulty is very individual — one player's wall is another's warm-up — so a single hard gate blocks a large fraction of players outright. Use both remedies: L010

  • Put brutal content in a separate optional layer. Tune the mandatory campaign to be completable. Bonus content is where hard resource limits belong.
  • Make progression non-linear. A level select, or a branching map, turns a wall into a detour instead of a dead stop.

Save your harshest devices for late or optional puzzles. Deep nesting — a puzzle whose inputs are an earlier puzzle's outputs — belongs at act endings. Hard cramping belongs in optional content. Never introduce both in the same new puzzle. L014

Whichever puzzle is currently last should be about the elegance of the route, not about whether it can be solved at all.

End on a sandbox

Close with a sandbox G029: every mechanic unlocked, the same rules, no target, and deliberately no score, so experimenting is not punished by metrics. Frame it in the fiction as something earned rather than offering it as a menu item. If players can share solutions, keep sandbox creations separate from ranked puzzle solutions. L015

You have a puzzle when you have written its spec and not its solution; when you can name in one word the bottleneck it exists to pressure; when the obvious build passes and scores badly; when you know of at least two architectures that finish it; and when you have checked that the time it takes is spent thinking rather than placing.

9 · Layer 5 · Optimization

Optimization is the second game, and it is what turns a game people finish into a game people keep. Completion accepts an honest, ugly machine. Afterwards the player is invited to compress it, speed it up, simplify it, or make it beautiful. L004 · P003

Splitting the two serves accessibility and mastery at once: solving is for everyone, optimizing is for whoever wants it.

A single best answer is a chore. A frontier of trade-offs is a hobby.

Choose metrics that fight each other

A metric G019 is a property measured on a solution that already passes. Several of them are not enough. They have to be antagonistic — improving one has to cost you another, or the player is optimizing a single thing under three names.

Nearly every game in the genre ships the same shape of triple: speed against resources against size. The names change with the domain, the structure does not.

Game Speed Resource Size
SpaceChem cycles symbols reactors
Opus Magnum cycles cost area
SHENZHEN I/O — cost, power lines of code
Infinifactory cycles blocks footprint
TIS-100 cycles instructions nodes
The Dye Bench cycles pigment wasted bench tiles

That is a strong default and you should have a reason to depart from it. L003

For each pair, ask: can improving one make the other worse? Do specialist solutions look structurally different? Is one metric just a proxy for another? Can a single build win every axis? The set of solutions where you cannot improve one axis without losing another is the Pareto frontier G022 — a good puzzle has a broad and populated one, and a puzzle with a single best answer on every axis has no second game in it at all.

The behavioural tell that your axes really conflict: players stop building balanced machines and start building one specialist per axis.

Show each metric separately. A weighted total hides exactly the trade-off you built.

Watch for decorative metrics. A metric that does not move across real solutions is a decoration, and you are scoring on one axis fewer than you claim. The usual case is a resource metric that only rises when a design wastes input — if slow is not wasteful and idle is not costly, resource stops conflicting with speed and size. Either make over-use tempting, or accept that you have a two-axis puzzle and say so.

On the Dye Bench. Measured on two real builds, cycles-against-tiles and cycles-against-waste both bite — and tiles-against-waste turns out to move the same way, because a bigger machine is a machine with more pigment stranded in it (§4). Two honest axes out of three is the normal result, and the third row is invisible until you go and measure it rather than argue it. The choice it forces is real: give waste teeth by charging for the taps, or stop advertising three axes.

Do this. Add operations whose costs do not move together — a big move that saves cycles but spikes energy — so the cheapest layout is not also the fastest.

Keep hard limits separate from scores

A hard limit G016 defines validity — exceed it and the puzzle is not solved. A score G030 invites refinement and decides nothing. Keeping that line sharp is what keeps the game finishable: turning a score into a gate narrows the solution space and can block progression, so use hard bounds only when the bound is the puzzle's explicit subject. Subjective qualities like elegance never decide correctness. Ugly solutions stay valid. P008

Make costs observable

Players optimize intelligently only when they can connect a score to behaviour. Show, where you can: when energy is spent, which operation adds latency, what expands the footprint, where capacity sits idle, which component creates waste, which instruction dominates the cycle count. P040

An opaque total supports trial and error. A causal cost model supports engineering.

Show a distribution, not a leaderboard

Per-puzzle histograms G017 — a chart of how many players scored what, with the player's own result marked on it. Leaderboards are a strong incentive to cheat, and they only tell a player they are behind. A histogram lets someone see impersonally where they sit — most players discover their solution is poor and immediately set themselves a target. L012

Show personal best, current score, improvements within reach, and visible clusters. Hide all of it until the player's first successful solve, so you do not anchor or spoil their architecture.

Aim for two humps

A second bump in the distribution — two humps G038 — is a design target, not an accident. It tells the player a completely different approach exists, which is a far stronger hook than "tweak what you have". You produce it deliberately, by authoring at least two decomposition families that land in different score regions (§8). A puzzle with one dominant approach is solved once and never reopened.

Never make them overwrite

Support several named solutions per puzzle, duplication and branching, a known-good baseline, metric-specific variants, thumbnails with recorded scores, and reliable import and export. If improving means destroying, players stop experimenting. P050

Make solutions shareable

A shared score says much less than a shared machine. An export should preserve the puzzle, the visible execution, readable timing, the relevant metrics, a stable frame, and an explicit spoiler action. Animated replays and inspectable builds are what turn private cleverness into a public culture — the single largest multiplier on a finished campaign's life. P049 · M114

Answer "why optimize" in the fiction

Give the metrics a reason to exist in the world: production economics, a factory under an overseer, speed before detection, elegance as a professional ideal, self-discipline in isolation. Frame comparison as peers, not as an unreachable authority. This is the point where optimization and story are the same design decision.

You have an optimization game when you can argue pair by pair that your metrics conflict; when the best solution on each axis looks structurally different from the others; when a player can tell why their score is what it is by watching the machine; and when improving a solution does not mean destroying the one that worked.

10 · Presentation · around every layer

Presentation is visual design, sound, animation, interface, editing, feedback, documentation, and terminology. It does not create depth. It reveals, amplifies, or hides it.

The dividing line is simple. A decorative skin is presentation. A visible port, capacity slot, timing trace, or collision boundary is part of play. Representation becomes mechanical the moment it changes what the player can perceive, predict, build, or debug.

That is also why comprehension problems get fixed here rather than in prose. If you find yourself rewording a part description for the third time, the fix is representational.

This is the layer beginners defer, on the reasonable-sounding grounds that it is the art pass and the art pass comes last. What actually happens is that the game becomes untestable. Nobody — including you — can tell a design problem from a legibility problem, so every playtest returns "I was confused" and no playtest tells you why.

On the Dye Bench. Before anything runs, a paused screenshot has to answer: which way does each pipe flow, how full is the vat, is the mixer working or waiting, and where did that unit of red come from? Every one of those is a drawing decision, and getting them right removes several paragraphs of manual that would otherwise have to exist (§4).

Make causality visible

The third column is the useful one. Each of these is written as something you can run against a build — take a screenshot, hand it to someone who has not seen the game, and see whether they can answer the question. Nine rules, nine tests; work down them once when you have something on screen.

Rule What it requires Review test ID
Every part shows its verb Shape, ports, and animation suggest whether it moves, rotates, stores, splits, joins, senses, or transforms. Related parts share a visual grammar. Without labels, can a new observer guess what a part does, and group its siblings correctly? P015 · P022
Never colour alone Combine colour with shape, texture, label, position, icon, motion, or connection type. Does it still work in grayscale? P016
Direction and connection at rest Ports G024 — the marked points where things enter and leave — wires, and conveyors show direction before anything runs. Connections are continuous, with explicit endpoints. In a paused screenshot, can a player trace a path, and tell connected from crossing from merely adjacent? P017 · P018
Separate material flow from control flow Show them as distinct but linked layers — spatial view for matter or data, timeline or code view for control — with selection bridging them. Can a player select a controller and see both its program and the hardware it drives? P024
Show state locally Active, waiting, blocked, holding, powered, full, empty, faulted — on or beside the part, never in a distant panel. At a paused tick, can the player tell what each component is doing or waiting for? P020
Fix transformation sites Where objects change type or relationship, the place and its preconditions are explicit and static. Can the player point at where every transformation happens? P021
Preserve object identity An item is traceable from source to destination and through transformation; motion follows the topology the player authored. Can the eye follow one object across a whole cycle without consulting a log? P025 · P063
Keep visual and semantic scale aligned Semantic zoom G031: simplify detail when zoomed out, but keep identity, connection, direction, state, and faults. At overview zoom, can the player see the architecture? At working zoom, can they read state? P019 · P056

Decorative minimalism is not a substitute for structured information. Technical density is fine if it grows progressively and stays organised: group by function, collapse advanced panes, keep a strong grid. P053

Editing

The time between an idea and its first observable consequence should be short. P004

  • Common edits are one gesture. Place, delete, rotate, connect, change an instruction, repeat. Sticky tools where repeated placement is likely. No dialog boxes for routine changes. P027
  • Revise without demolishing. Region select, move, copy and paste, rotate or mirror, shift paths, retime sections, deep undo and redo. Open-ended design is refactoring; forcing reconstruction punishes learning. P028
  • Keep context across modes. Switching between workspace, documentation, test cases, code, and metrics should preserve selection, camera, zoom, partial edits, and simulation state. P030
  • Predict illegality before commit. Ghost the part, mark affected cells and ports, show reach arcs and collision footprints, explain the rejection where it happens. P026
  • Allow notes that do not execute. Labels, comments, coloured regions — quieter than real parts, hideable, so a player can understand their own solution a week later. P031

Running and debugging

  • Let partial machines run. Incomplete and wrong constructions execute whenever the simulator can produce meaningful behaviour. Missing output is an unmet spec, not an editor error. P064
  • Full transport controls, always reachable. Run, pause, reset, single-step. Several speeds: slow enough to inspect, normal to reason, fast to test a mature machine, plus a clean speed for watching finished work. Single-step advances exactly one unit of time. P033 · P034
  • Connect control to consequence. When an instruction fires, highlight both it and the thing it affects. Hovering either highlights the other. Keep the tick, the test case, and the phase visible. P035
  • Waiting and blocking are states, not crashes. Blocking G003 is a part that cannot act yet holding its place. Show what is waiting, what it is waiting for, which resource is occupied, and whether the system is deadlocked G010 or merely slow. P036
  • Diagnose the first fault, not the last. Identify the earliest real violation, preserve the failing state, and line up expected against actual output in one coordinate system. A readable trace G037 of what happened, step by step, is the difference between "it failed" and "it failed here, because of this". Cascading later faults must not bury the first divergence. P038 · P039
  • Failure is spectacle, not punishment. Make crashes enjoyable to watch but fast, local, informative, and costless. The effect should centre on the offending part rather than cover it. P065

Trust and access

  • Keep the working set available. Goal, workspace, controls, and current scores get cross-referenced constantly. They should be visible at once, or reachable without losing context. P052
  • Selection is the universal gesture. Selecting anything reveals its properties, state, program, connections, and current contribution to failure or cost. One gesture, every object. P054
  • Pair symbols with words. Icon plus label until mastery is plausible. One stable term per part, state, port, and operation — in the interface, the manual, the tutorial, and the error messages. Flavourful names sit beside exact ones, never replace them. P059
  • Scale the density. Adjustable text and UI scale, adjustable simulation speed, reduced motion, inspectable paused state, colour-vision-safe distinctions. No required information exists only in fast animation, sound, or colour. P057 · P058
  • Be an impartial simulator. Deterministic replay, stable terminology, explicit constraints, visible ordering, reproducible scoring, no hidden preferences, no unexplained exceptions. When a player fails, the system should feel strict but fair. P060

The closing rule. When authentic decoration and operational clarity conflict, keep the fiction but let the operation win. The player should always be able to tell what a part does, where things go, what is happening now, and why the run failed.

Your presentation works when someone who has never seen the game can look at a paused screenshot and describe what the machine is trying to do; when a failed run points at the first thing that went wrong rather than the last; when the common edits are one gesture each; and when you have stopped needing to explain a part in words because its face explains it.

11 · Teaching and progression

The player is learning one system. Individual puzzles are the vehicle. Progress is fluency, not levels cleared: predicting behaviour before running, recognising reusable structures, diagnosing failures quickly, choosing an architecture on purpose. You want them to move from how do I solve this? to I understand this system well enough to design a solution.

The campaign G008 — the ordered run of puzzles that forms the main game — is therefore a curriculum whether you treat it as one or not. Treating it as one is cheaper, because the alternative is discovering the curriculum by watching players fall off it.

Start in the real editor

The first interaction uses the real tools and the real rules. A small meaningful build teaches the interface better than a disposable tutorial mode, because everything the player learns transfers. Constrain the task, pre-place some context, introduce Run, Step, and Reset, then take the scaffolding away. P041

One new thing at a time

Introduce exactly one new device, law, or interaction, in a context where it is the only unknown. Then combine it with everything already learned.

The failure mode is precise and common: difficulty spikes happen when combinations outpace teaching. When a curriculum has a wall, look first for a puzzle that debuted two interacting ideas in one breath. L002

The shape runs across a cluster of puzzles, not a single one:

Step Role
Introduce The new idea is the only unknown, in a safe, low-stakes puzzle
Develop The idea works under real metric or constraint pressure
Twist The idea recombines with something learned earlier
Conclude The player shows independent mastery

Do this. For every new part, author one puzzle whose solution needs only that part's idea. Never debut two interacting mechanics in the same brief.

On the Dye Bench. Backpressure is emergent, not authored, which makes it easy to forget that it still has to be taught. So one early puzzle exists purely to jam: a target that the obvious build cannot reach without a vat filling up, on a bench where the jam is impossible to miss. Nothing else is new in it. By the time backpressure can cost the player a real puzzle, they have already met it somewhere it cost them nothing (§4).

If your system allows it, let solved components become placeable parts. Then every puzzle layers one idea onto trusted black boxes, and modularity is the curriculum.

Teach in the puzzle and the manual, not in prose

A new part should explain itself through its own face, through a puzzle shape that makes its purpose obvious, and through visible consequences. If a mechanic cannot be taught that way, the teaching is unbuilt — fix the representation instead of adding a paragraph. L013

The rest is carried by in-world reference material, treated as a real design surface: datasheets, manuals, zines, reference cards. It should be searchable or well indexed, use the same terms as the interface, be rich in diagrams, be available without losing work, and be explicit about ordering and edge cases. Worked examples clarify syntax without becoming mandatory templates. P046

Making the manual an in-world object turns reading the rules into inhabiting the role. It is why documentation-heavy onboarding, which ought to be hostile, is one of the genre's signature pleasures.

Teach edge cases before they punish

Blocking, simultaneous actions, collision order, overflow, capacity, and test timing must be demonstrated before they cause a major failure. Players may discover strategies. They should never have to discover undocumented semantics. P045

Constrain early, open late

Early puzzles: small toolsets, readable workspaces, few concepts at once, little optimization pressure. As fluency grows, expand the palette, the workspace, concurrency, nesting, and the optimization surface. P066

Advanced tools can be available early as long as they are clearly marked optional rather than hidden. That lets experts optimize immediately while beginners still see a suggested subset.

Set the solve/optimize balance per puzzle, not once for the game. Early puzzles can sit almost entirely in solving, with near-zero optimization surface, and that is correct. Do not bolt fake scoring pressure onto a puzzle that cannot support a second life.

Give them a break

A low-pressure side activity restores attention if it is optional, thematically coherent, mechanically lighter, quick to enter and leave, and separate from required progress. The genre convention is a diegetic G013 solitaire or toy — one that exists inside the fiction, something the character owns rather than a menu item the game offers. Cheap to build, disproportionately good for tone, and readable as the character slacking off at work. It should renew appetite for the main system, not compete with it. P069 · M133

Your teaching works when every part has one puzzle where it is the only unknown; when no brief debuts two interacting ideas at once; when the semantics that can punish a player — blocking, ordering, overflow, collision — have all been demonstrated somewhere harmless first; and when a player who is stuck has somewhere else to go.

12 · Story and framing

Story earns its place for three practical reasons: it gives the puzzles something to be about, it answers design questions for you (§5), and it answers why optimize (§9). It is also cheaper than you think — a portrait and written mail cost almost nothing.

Puzzle players do not skip fiction. The one published figure in the genre found roughly 75% of demo players engaging with the story.

If you are building alone and wondering what to cut, this chapter is the one that most often gets cut and most often should not. Nothing here asks for a writer, a cutscene, or a voice budget. The default in this genre is a few hundred words in somebody's voice, attached to the work you were already going to describe.

Where story sits

  1. It rides the task brief. The mandatory channel is the brief G006 itself — the puzzle assignment, written in a character's voice. Multiple developers arrived at this independently. The brief sits at the edge of the loop, before the build, so it never interrupts construction. P068
  2. Everything else is optional and outside the loop. Logs you can walk past, dialogue behind an icon, zines read between sessions, post-solve lines framed as a reward. No surveyed game has an unskippable cutscene between puzzles, and every documented failure is about forced or confusing story, never optional story.
  3. Post-victory dialogue is the validated slot. It advances the plot without cluttering the workspace, is trivially skippable, and reads as a reward. A hub that asks the player to pause between puzzles fights the loop, which wants flow.

How much

Find the row you can afford, then read the paragraph under the table — the rung you pick matters less than spending inside the fiction rather than beside it. The bolded row is where most of this genre lives, and it is achievable by one person in a week.

Tier What it costs
A premise sentence and flavour text, no characters Nothing — and consistently noticed as a lack
Diegetic notes and vignettes, one to three named characters Hundreds to a couple of thousand words

Portrait dialogue, voiced logs, staged scenes Voice acting is a low single-digit percentage of a full budget, and has returned well

The ladder matters more than the tiers. Spend inside the fiction's own medium. The one expensive experiment that did not return — a long standalone graphic novel bundled with an unrelated game — failed because it sat outside the interface, not because it was long.

The floor is higher than it looks. A bare ribbon of a thousand words across a whole campaign lands in the territory reviewers file as set dressing. If you are keeping prose scarce, something physical or diegetic has to do the compensating work: a printable manual, a distinctive audiovisual whole, a handwritten letter.

Three habits worth having

  • Write in two tiers. Mandatory beats stay short; deep lore is opt-in. The optional tier is where the most-loved material lives — printable zines, hidden logs, sealed envelopes — and it is what generates community archaeology. The curious get a second, stranger story; the impatient lose nothing mechanical.
  • Make everything re-readable. The one direct experiment with unrepeatable post-solve text failed its players, who edited save files to recover it. Decide where solved puzzles' briefs and post-solve lines live afterwards.
  • Put the writer in the room when puzzles are chosen. Then briefs stop being decoration and start generating puzzle ideas the systems designer would not have reached.

If the brief has to teach and carry voice inside a hard word budget, write down which one yields. The answer should be that teaching load moves to representation and the manual (§11) rather than eating the voice.

The genre's house themes, if you want them: labour under institutions; obsolescence and legacy; found community at the margins; and above all the melancholy of competence — being very good at a job that is lonely, exploited, or unwitnessed, and finding meaning in the craft anyway.

You have a story when you can say who the player works for and why they cannot walk away; when one in-world medium carries both the teaching and the voice; when nothing mandatory interrupts the build–run–inspect–edit loop; and when every word of it can be read again later.

The workflow

  1. Place it. Which act does it belong to, and what does the player already know?
  2. Name the one new thing, or the one bottleneck. If the answer is "the last puzzle, but bigger", cut it.
  3. Write the spec, not the solution. Fix inputs, target, quota, board, palette, limits first.
  4. Confirm the obvious route works, and that it scores badly.
  5. Find at least one real reframing that changes the architecture or the metrics.
  6. Verify it is solvable. Reference builds, search, or a solver. Keep a verified reference so behaviour is canonical.
  7. Check the metrics move. Test each pair on real solutions. If one does not move, add a temptation that makes it move, or declare the puzzle two-axis.
  8. Cut the labour. Remove work that remains after the insight has landed.
  9. Ship it provisional. Its position is a hypothesis. Watch it solved, then reorder, bridge, widen, tighten, or cut.

Playtesting

You cannot evaluate your own game — you know too much. Test as soon as anything is interactive.

Give only the instructions a real player would get. Watch what people do rather than what they say. Note hesitation and ignored features. Ask neutral questions. Do not explain or defend. A player's proposed fix may be wrong, but the confusion they felt is real evidence. Change one major thing at a time, and decide what success looks like before you test.

Analytics find patterns. Playtests explain them.

Build these alongside the puzzles

Individual puzzles assume these exist. Authoring for a system that never arrives is wasted work.

  • Per-metric histograms — the second-life engine. Puzzles authored for two humps have nothing to show without it.
  • Clean-looping replay or animation export — the social artifact that turns optimization into culture.
  • A solver over your puzzle's problem space — turns "author a reference solution" into "map the whole frontier", and lets you compute what other games can only playtest.
  • Named solution branches — without them, optimizing means overwriting, and players stop experimenting.

You are working well when the one-page definition exists and you have argued with it at least once; when no puzzle gets implemented before its sheet is filled in; when you have watched someone else play in the last fortnight; and when the four systems above exist in rough form rather than being scheduled for later.

15 · The final check

Run this against a build, not against a document. Every item is something you can watch a player do or fail to do.

It is a long list and it is meant to be. Nothing here is a matter of taste — each line is either true of your game or it is not, and each one that is not points at a specific chapter. Work down it with the game running in front of you, and expect the first pass to fail several items; that is what it is for.

  • Can the player point at the machine they made?
  • Can radically different valid architectures pass?
  • Can an imperfect solution complete a puzzle?
  • Is every hard limit visible on screen?
  • Does every part show its verb, direction, capacity, and state?
  • Can material, data, and control be traced?
  • Can common and structural edits be made cheaply?
  • Can partial machines run?
  • Are run, pause, reset, step, and speed always available?
  • Can the player diagnose waiting, blocking, and the first fault?
  • Are expected and actual output directly comparable?
  • Can a score be connected to the behaviour that caused it?
  • Do the best solutions per metric look structurally different?
  • Are score distributions hidden until the first solve?
  • Is terminology identical across every surface?
  • Is required information available without colour, sound, or motion?
  • Can a stuck player continue somewhere else?
  • Can a finished machine be watched, kept, branched, and shared?

The standard. The player should be able to understand each rule, trace each consequence, build something that is recognisably theirs, and still be surprised by what the rules permit together.

The guiding rule. Build the smallest game that fully delivers that experience, then improve it by watching people play rather than by assuming.

How to read a row

The mechanics register is the dense one, so it is worth walking a row of it once. Take M002, multiple or adversarial test cases:

  • Description — what the rule is: a solution is replayed against varied inputs.
  • Player action — what it makes them do: generalize the logic instead of hard-coding.
  • Constraints — what it costs them: unknown future cases; one failure invalidates everything.
  • Optimization axes — what it gives you to score: robustness, worst-case cycles, code size.
  • Emergence — low, medium or high. How much behaviour arises that nobody authored.
  • Games using it — best-effort attribution, not an exhaustive list.
  • Combines with — the rows that change what this one means. This is the column that matters.

Read that last column first. A mechanic's value is almost never in its own row — it is in how many other rows it changes, which is the argument §6 makes and the reason a combines with list is short for a shallow mechanic.

These are not a specification. A couple of hundred rows are listed across the registers. That describes the genre; it does not describe your game. Every good game here runs on a small, coherent handful, and the difference between a design and a pile is which rows you left out (§6).

L001Design the problem, never the solutionAuthor a spec and deliberately no solution; the engine validates output, never method. Spec-first also avoids the chicken-and-egg problem of designing a puzzle and its answer at once.Write the target and palette first, then check several genuinely different decompositions validate.§8
L002One new mechanic per puzzle, isolated then combinedIntroduce exactly one new device, law, or interaction where it is the only unknown, then combine it with everything already learned. Difficulty spikes occur when combinations outpace teaching.For every new part, author one puzzle whose solution requires only that part's idea.§11
L003The metrics must genuinely conflictMultiple metrics are not enough; they must be antagonistic. A well-designed puzzle has a broad, populated Pareto frontier rather than a single best.Confirm each pair of metrics trades off on real solutions; add non-monotone cost profiles if one does not move.§9
L004Solving is for everyone; optimizing is the second gameSplit difficulty into two regimes: reaching any valid output should be broadly achievable, and climbing the histogram carries the hardcore challenge.Tune each puzzle so a messy, honest solution passes, then let the metrics carry the depth.§9
L005The insight is a fair reframing, not an unguessable trickA fair puzzle hides a reframing of constraints the player already holds, composed from primitives already taught, and makes complete sense in hindsight. Mix Aha! puzzles with Process puzzles.Let the naïve decomposition pass but score poorly; place the elegant reframing where the mediocre score pushes the player to look.§8
L006Guarantee a solution exists; never ship “the” solutionUnbounded resources make a puzzle solvable by construction; bounded resources make solvability something you must verify. Ship the guarantee, never an answer key.Verify by reference construction, search, or DP, and map the actual frontier across the metrics.§8
L007Vary the bottleneck, not the numbersEach puzzle should pressure a different facet of the system so it provokes a new architecture rather than a longer copy of the last one. Raising a quota is the classic false difficulty.Name the bottleneck before building. If the answer is “an earlier one, but more,” change the facet or cut it.§8
L008The board is the primary difficulty dialBounded-and-cramped, open canvas, and graph/network boards each place difficulty somewhere different. Boundedness is what keeps a puzzle game from decaying into a sandbox.Set board and palette to place the solve/optimize split deliberately. Tighten space to force a cleverer decomposition, never a tighter squeeze of the obvious one.§8
L009Order empirically: measure, de-duplicate, bridgeDifficulty order is edited after observation, not authored by intuition. Three operations fall out: reorder by measured difficulty, delete logical duplicates, insert bridges.Never freeze a puzzle's position before it has been watched being solved.§8
L010Keep the spikes off the critical pathOpen-ended puzzles produce highly individual difficulty, so one hard gate blocks a large fraction of players. Siphon brutal content into a separated optional layer and make the structure non-linear.Tune the mandatory campaign to be completable; mark the hardest challenges optional. The duty travels with whichever puzzle is currently last.§8
L011Protect determinism; separate insight cost from execution costNever smuggle real-time pressure, randomness, or dexterity in as difficulty. The subtler trap is execution cost masquerading as difficulty when the idea lands in a minute and the layout takes an hour.Measure the shape of solve time, not only its length; improve structural editing or reshape the puzzle.§8
L012Author for the histogram, and build itThe per-puzzle histogram, not a global leaderboard, converts a beatable campaign into an endless one. A bimodal distribution is a design target, not an accident.Design some puzzles to produce two decomposition families landing in different score regions; sequence the histogram and export systems with the levels.§9
L013Teach in the puzzle and the reference, not a tutorialTeach by having the player use the mechanic, and offload the rest onto in-world reference material treated as a core design surface. A rule a well-formed system can show should not be told in a text box.If a new part cannot be taught by puzzle shape plus a self-displaying face, the teaching is unbuilt — fix the representation, do not add a paragraph.§11
L014Cramp and nest only in optional or late contentResource-cramping is where “tight and clever” tips into “tight and unfair”; nesting is the genre's emotional peak but raises cognitive load steeply.Keep early and mid puzzles to one transformation stage on a comfortable board. Never introduce cramping and nesting in the same new puzzle.§8
L015End on a sandbox, framed as graduationA final unconstrained space with every mechanic unlocked, no target, and deliberately no score, framed diegetically as earned rather than offered as a menu item.Preserve the sandbox as the unscored graduation lap; share sandbox creations separately from ranked solutions.§8

E · Games to learn from

Where these principles came from. Calibration, not a recipe — play them, and read the column that matters.

If you are new to the genre, start with the second column and pick two that sound unlike each other; §1 suggests one generous and one starved. If you already know these games, the fourth column is the one with the argument in it — each row is the lesson this guide took from that game, including where the game got it wrong.

Infinifactory Build 3D assembly lines from conveyors, welders and pushers, walking around inside your own factory Open 3D chamber · cycles / footprint / blocks Difficulty order fixed by measured playtest, not intuition

Patterns that recur across all of them

  • Scarcity of trivial resources — registers, lines, space, parts. The puzzle is rarely the task; it is the poverty.
  • Two overlapping representations — the program is also a picture.
  • Blocking communication — synchronisation doubles as puzzle content.
  • Solve, then optimize — correctness is the tutorial, the metrics are the game.
  • Backpressure as feedback — systems fail by visibly clogging, not by error codes.
  • One new element per level, immediately combined with everything already known.
  • The community metric — histograms and shared animations turn private cleverness into public culture.
